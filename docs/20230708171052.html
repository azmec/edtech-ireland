Irish Educational Technology

Enterprise

As established in surveys of Ireland’s educational technology policy and research institutions, the industry’s stakeholders and their interests are diverse. Educational technology has been shown to, historically, been viewed as a catalyst for modernisation, a necessity for the global knowledge economy, and an enabler for transformative learning when aligned with practice. Appropriately, this diversity is seen in Ireland’s educational technology enterprises.

It was previously discussed the DES values educational technology for the ways it may transform and personalize assessment. Among these transformative modes of assessments are formative assessments, with which Irish enterprise JumpAGrade and Nurture are transforming the educational technology sector. Both enterprises were established by David Neville and Pádraic Hogan and well-situated within Ireland’s plain-education market. JumpAGrade, for instance, competes with Irish cram schools, called “grinds”, by offering an online tutoring service providing personalized assessment feedback. Nicholl, Ronan Rabbitts, Nick. “University of Limerick-Based Firm JumpAGrade Pioneers Online Exam Service.” Accessed July 27, 2023. https://www.limerickleader.ie/news/home/317403/university-of-limerick-based-firm-jumpagrade-pioneers-online-exam-service.html. Kennedy, John. “JumpAgrade: This Start-up Is the Modern Answer to Grinds.” Silicon Republic, August 13, 2018. https://www.siliconrepublic.com/start-ups/jumpagrade-grinds-education. In this way, JumpAGrade reflects the DES’ valuation of educational technology by directly integrating technology into existing, sound pedagogy: consistent, personalized feedback is good whether it is offline or digital. Neville and Hogan’s latest venture, Nurture, continues this trend of closing the feedback loop. Nurture offers an assessment platform facilitating instructors’ feedback at the task, process, and holistic level, facilitating the formative assessment pattern. In addition, students may report their confidence levels in tasks and fulfilling reflection forms with every task submission. Nicholl, Ronan Mc. “EdTech Assessment - Closing the Feedback Loop.” Global EdTech (blog), May 19, 2022. https://global-edtech.com/edtech-assessment-closing-the-feedback-loop/. Further, Nurture is research-backed by Learnovate, again demonstrating the research institutes influence on the educational technology sector. Nicholl, Ronan Nurture. “Our Framework Is Research-Backed | Nurture.” Nurture. Accessed July 9, 2023. https://gonurture.com//research. This is, once again, a digitization of already sound educational practice—a leveraging of technology to make once infeasible teaching strategies, offering personalized, self-regulated feedback to all students, feasible. Furthermore, Neville and Hogan continue to push the envelope by integrating this assessment platform with artificial intelligence by Open AI and Microsoft’s Teams platform. The Irish educational technology platform spans far and wide.

As the presence of the knowledge or information society was felt in the DES’ educational policy, it continues to ring in Ireland’s educational technology sector. Well before Learnovate re-aligned itself with Ireland’s learning-for-life framework, courseware enterprise Spotlight Skills was targeting this market. Spotlight Skills provides courseware instructing high-school students in workplace competencies such as interpersonal communication, relationship-building, and decision-making. Spotlight Skills. “Spotlight Skills | Schools.” Accessed June 25, 2023. https://www.spotlightskills.com/schools. Not only does the enterprise represent the economic interest of educational technology, it further demonstrates the influence the Irish government has on the sector. Spotlight Skills collaborated with Learnovate to develop the instruction and assessment tools composing its courseware, and the enterprise was supported by Enterprise Ireland. Ibid. Though educational technology is typically regarded as the application of virtual realities or games, it is clearly additionally seen as an opportunity to ensure students are competent in the workplace.

  • Game-based or interactive learning software exemplifies many of the principles of educational technology espoused by the DES in their policy. In addition, it is perhaps the broadest domain of educational technology.

It must be recognized educational technology enterprises rely on a diversity of skill-sets and industries to compose their products. The nascent mobile application enterprise Little Red Edu is most exemplary of this. Little Red Edu, founded by Anna Carmody, is a mobile application delivering gamified English-language learning lessons through a subscription-based service. Children repeat or read-aloud English words or phrases, and the application assesses their pronunciation. Anna, Carmody. “Our Story.” Little Red Edu. Accessed June 25, 2023.https://littlerededu.com/about-our-story/. Here, the “technology” of the educational technology product amounts to an automated assessment system and game-elements, reflecting a facet of the DES’ policy. Of note, however, is the reliance the application has on Soapbox Labs, a Learnovate spin-in, and subsequent spin-out, providing a low-code interface to voice recognition technology trained on child voice data. Little Red Edu, then, straddles the domains of pedagogy, visual design, game design, and, as a dependency, machine learning. Further, it is reliant on the research performed by the Learnovate Centre insofar as Soapbox Labs remains under the research institute, demonstrating the role the institute plays in the educational technology sector.

Father of education John Dewey forever criticized the “traditional scheme” of education, wherein the information and skills of the past generation are imposed onto the nascent minds of the current generations’ students without regard for their experiences. The gap in the experience required to digest the subject-matter and the experience held by the students is so great that the material is foreign. Dewey, John. “Experience and Education.” The Educational Forum 50, no. 3 (September 30, 1986): 241–52. https://doi.org/10.1080/00131728609335764. Virtual reality (VR) proposes to facilitate students’ acquiring of the experiences necessary to close the gap. Virtual reality implies a movement from text-based instruction to symbol-based instruction, giving students the experiences necessary to make sense of foreign subject-matters. Helsel, Sandra. “Virtual Reality and Education.” Educational Technology 32, no. 5 (1992): 38–42. Gee, James Paul. What Video Games Have to Teach Us About Learning and Literacy. 1. paperback ed. Education. New York, NY: Palgrave Macmillan, 2004. However, VR application in education has largely been limited to specialized simulation or training sequences and, consequentially, are yet to be regarded as a general medium of instruction. Kavanagh, Sam, Andrew Luxton-Reilly, Burkhard Wuensche, and Beryl Plimmer. “A Systematic Review of Virtual Reality in Education.” Themes in Science and Technology Education 10, no. 2 (December 27, 2017): 85–119. Irish educational technology enterprise SchooVR is shifting this matter.

Founded by Mark Baldwin, SchooVR provides teachers and students a platform to build educational VR experiences and a library of VR course-content. At the individual level, Baldwin’s conception of the role VR technology has to play in education is well-aligned with that of the DES. When interviewed by the Irish Times, Baldwin claimed, “Today’s ‘digital natives’ need more than words on a page”, Keogh, Olive. “Teacher’s Virtual Reality Platform Aims to Improve Student Engagement.” The Irish Times, January 17, 2019. https://www.irishtimes.com/business/innovation/teacher-s-virtual-reality-platform-aims-to-improve-student-engagement-1.3754661. echoing the DES’ Investing Effectively claim students’ technological ecosystem has fundamentally changed learning and directly lifting Mark Prensky’s digital native concept. This demonstrates the perspective of educational technology wherein it must be aligned with learner’s experiences (i.e., learner-centered). Further, in alignment with the DES’ policy for instructors to be competent in the tools necessary to enact policy, educational institutions are using SchooVR tools and content to train student-teachers. In 2021, the Trinity College of Dublin invited student-teachers to participate in a course teaching the use of immersive technologies in the classroom. Farrell, Rachel. “Immersive Technology in Education.” University College of Dublin: Teaching & Learning. Accessed July 28, 2023. https://www.ucd.ie/teaching/showcase/immersivetechnologyineducation/. Baldwin’s SchooVR presents is a concise demonstration of the not only the presence of VR in Ireland’s educational technology industry but also its intersecting with educational institutions and the DES’ policy.

Yet, there continues to be a need to progress the conception of game-based learning, particularly in the mode of integrating game-design principles and ensuring instructors possess the competencies necessary to develop and use games. Irish enterprise Jetpack Learning fulfills the latter half of the bargain, providing a platform to build HTML-5-based games. Jetpack Learning. “Jetpack Learning | HTML5 Game-Based Learning Authoring Tool.” Accessed July 28, 2023. https://www.jetpacklearning.com/. Though Learnovate’s influence is once-more witnessed in Jetpack Learning’s collaboration with the research-institute, Jetpack Learning. “Jetpack Team up with Trinity College Dublin’s Learnovate Centre | Jetpack Learning,” October 8, 2019. https://www.jetpacklearning.com/jetpack-team-up-with-learnovate/. a survey of the enterprise’s showcased games demonstrates an unfortunate integration of game design principles and aesthetics. Dickey (2019) argued for the necessity of instructors to become familiar with game-design principles on the ground that instructors are already in the business of designing aesthetic experiences and, most important, the benefit of game-based learning emerges from a systemic application of such principles. Dickey, Michele D. Aesthetics and Design for Game-Based Learning. Digital Games and Learning. New York: Routledge, Taylor & Francis Group, 2015. If game-based learning is to progress seriously within educational technologies, instructors must not only possess the tools to develop content-relevant games but also be competent with games as a medium of expression.
